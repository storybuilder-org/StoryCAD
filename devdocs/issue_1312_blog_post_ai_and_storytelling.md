# Where We Stand: AI and the Future of Storytelling

**StoryBuilder Foundation believes stories belong to the people who tell them. Here's what that means as AI changes the landscape.**

---

Every story begins with a human being who has something to say. A question that won't let go. A character who shows up uninvited. A what-if that demands to be followed. That impulse — the need to tell a story — is as old as language itself, and no technology is going to replace it.

But technology is changing the conversation around it, and we think it's time we told you where we stand.

If you're a fiction writer paying attention right now, you've probably felt a mix of curiosity, frustration, and genuine worry about artificial intelligence. You're not wrong to feel any of those things. The questions writers are asking — about ownership, about craft, about whether the machines are coming for your job — are serious questions that deserve serious answers, not hand-waving or hype.

The publishing industry is wrestling with this in real time. The Science Fiction and Fantasy Writers Association now asks authors to disclose AI use in award submissions. The Authors Guild has taken legal action against AI companies over the use of copyrighted works in training data. Publishers are drawing lines too — Penguin Random House added language to its copyright pages affirming that its books weren't produced by AI, and Hachette has introduced disclosure requirements for submissions. These aren't overreactions. They're institutions trying to protect the value of human creative work, and we respect that.

We're a small nonprofit that makes a free outlining tool for fiction writers. We're not a publishing house or a tech giant. But we are people who care deeply about storytelling, and we think even small organizations have a responsibility to be clear about their values when the ground is shifting.

So here's what we believe.

**Your story is yours.** When you use StoryCAD to develop an outline, build characters, map out your plot structure — that work belongs to you. Full stop. We don't claim any ownership over what you create. We never have, and we never will. That's not a negotiating position. It's a founding principle.

**Human creativity is the point.** StoryCAD has always been about giving writers better tools to do the hard, rewarding work of shaping a story. We think of it like drafting software for architects — it helps you see the structure, test the load-bearing walls, figure out where the rooms go. But the vision is yours. The choices are yours. The voice is yours. We're interested in tools that make you more capable, not tools that do the work for you.

**We don't pretend the world is simple.** We'd be dishonest if we told you that AI exists in a clean, uncomplicated space. The large language models that power modern AI tools were trained on enormous datasets drawn from the open internet, and those datasets likely include published creative works. That's an industry-wide reality, not something any single company can opt out of. It's also the subject of active litigation and ongoing policy debate, and we think writers deserve to know that rather than have it glossed over.

What we can tell you is what we control. Your outlines live on your machine. When you use our AI features, specific story elements are sent to the AI service to generate suggestions. We don't use your data to train anything, and we don't index copyrighted books or stories. You remain in control of your work.

**We also believe in your responsibility as a professional.** If you submit work to a publisher, an agent, a contest, or a market, it's on you to understand their policies around AI-assisted content. Those policies vary, they're evolving fast, and they matter. We'll always be transparent about what our tools do and don't do, but we can't make submission decisions for you. Respect the guidelines of wherever you send your work.

We know some writers want nothing to do with AI, and we respect that completely. StoryCAD has worked without it for years and will continue to. Others are curious about where these tools might genuinely help — not with the writing itself, but with the thinking that comes before writing. The brainstorming. The structural problem-solving. The "what if I moved this scene here" kind of work that every writer does, usually alone, usually at two in the morning.

We've been thinking about that kind of help for a while now. We'll have more to share soon.

In the meantime, we wanted you to hear it from us directly: we build tools for writers, we are writers, and we believe the future of storytelling is human. Everything we make will reflect that.
