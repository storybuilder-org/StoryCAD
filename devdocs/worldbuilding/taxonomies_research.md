# Worldbuilding Taxonomies and Frameworks Research

**Research Date:** January 13, 2026
**Purpose:** Comprehensive analysis of worldbuilding category taxonomies used in fiction writing

---

## Executive Summary

This research identifies and analyzes worldbuilding frameworks from 25+ sources spanning professional authors, writing craft organizations, game design, and academic perspectives. The findings reveal consistent patterns across sources while highlighting variations in organizational approaches.

### Key Finding: Common Taxonomy Categories

Most sources organize worldbuilding into 7-12 major categories. The most universally recognized categories are:

1. **Physical/Geography** - Terrain, climate, natural features
2. **Cultural/Social** - Customs, traditions, values
3. **Political/Governmental** - Power structures, laws
4. **Economic** - Trade, resources, currency
5. **Historical** - Timeline, major events
6. **Religious/Spiritual** - Beliefs, deities, practices
7. **Magic/Technology** - Rule-based systems
8. **People/Species** - Inhabitants, races
9. **Language/Communication** - Linguistic elements
10. **Daily Life** - Food, clothing, customs

## Part 1: Major Frameworks and Taxonomies

### 1.1 Patricia C. Wrede's Fantasy Worldbuilding Questions (SFWA)

**Source:** [Fantasy Worldbuilding Questions - SFWA](https://www.sfwa.org/2009/08/04/fantasy-worldbuilding-questions/)

**Overview:** The most comprehensive and widely-referenced worldbuilding framework, originally published by the Science Fiction and Fantasy Writers Association. Organized into 7 major categories with extensive subcategories.

**Taxonomy Structure:**

| Major Category | Subcategories |
|----------------|---------------|
| **1. The World** | Basics, Alternate Earth, Not Earth at All |
| **2. Physical and Historical Features** | General, Climate and Geography, Natural Resources, World History, Specific Country History |
| **3. Magic and Magicians** | Rules of Magic, Wizards, Magic and Technology, Miscellaneous Magic Questions |
| **4. Peoples and Customs** | General, Customs, Eating, Greeting and Meeting, Gestures, Visits, Language, Ethics and Values, Religion and the Gods, Population |
| **5. Social Organization** | General, Government, Politics, Crime and Legal System, Foreign Relations, Waging War, Weapons |
| **6. Commerce, Trade, and Public Life** | General, Business and Industry, Transportation and Communication, Science and Technology, Medicine, Arts and Entertainment, Architecture, Urban Factors, Rural Factors |
| **7. Daily Life** | General, Fashion and Dress, Manners, Diet, Education, Calendar |

**Notable Insight:** "It is not necessary for an author to answer all, or even any, of the questions in order to start writing. The idea is simply to provoke people into thinking about the ways their settings and backgrounds hang together."

### 1.2 Brandon Sanderson's Worldbuilding Approach (2025)

**Source:** [Worldbuilding Tools from Brandon Sanderson | Lecture Notes 2025](https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/worldbuilding-tools-lecture-2025)

**Overview:** Sanderson emphasizes practical, story-driven worldbuilding with strategic focus areas rather than exhaustive detail.

**Key Framework Elements:**

**Three Pillars:**
- Setting (physical world)
- Plot (events and story)
- Character (inhabitants and motivations)

**Physical vs. Cultural Division:**
- **Physical Setting:** "All the stuff that would exist even if there were no sentient beings on the planet doing things"
- **Cultural Setting:** Elements created by inhabitants

**Sanderson's Third Law of Worldbuilding:** "Expand what you have before you add something new."

**Recommended Focus:**
- One physical element (deep development)
- No more than two cultural elements (detailed exploration)

**Warning - Worldbuilder's Disease:** "You could spend so long on this that you get paralyzed by the amount that you have to do."

**Practical Example:** "In Mistborn, the languages weren't crucial so everyone spoke a common language. In Stormlight on the other hand, the language was very vital for my story and I developed the linguistics."

**Source:** [Brandon Sanderson's World-building Tips - LivingWriter](https://livingwriter.com/blog/brandon-sandersons-world-building-tips/)

### 1.4 Scribophile's Nine Essential Elements

**Source:** [Worldbuilding: The Ultimate Guide - Scribophile](https://www.scribophile.com/academy/what-is-worldbuilding)

**World Types:**
1. **Primary Worlds** - Real-world settings enhanced with new elements (e.g., Harry Potter)
2. **Secondary Worlds** - Entirely original universes (e.g., Game of Thrones)
3. **Alternate Histories** - Familiar worlds altered by "What if?" scenarios

**Nine Essential Elements:**

| Element | Focus Areas |
|---------|-------------|
| People | Physical characteristics, abilities, races, evolution |
| Geography | Terrain, climate, landscapes, maps |
| Ecosystem | Flora, fauna, weather, natural disasters, seasons |
| Language | Vocabulary, grammar, phonetic consistency, written systems |
| Economy | Currency systems, resources, trade, wealth distribution |
| Culture & Religion | Beliefs, traditions, ceremonies, holidays, rituals |
| Social Construction | Power systems, laws, class structure, governance |
| Soft Magic/Technology | Unexplained systems integrated into daily life |
| Hard Magic/Technology | Meticulously detailed, rule-based systems |

**Three Common Mistakes:**
- Borrowing too heavily from existing fictional worlds
- Overwhelming readers with excessive exposition
- Including disparate elements lacking coherent connection

### 1.6 R.M. Archer's Four Pillars of Culture

**Source:** [Elements of Culture for Worldbuilding - Scribes & Archers](https://rmarcher.com/2021/06/elements-of-culture-for-worldbuilding/)

**Framework:** Four primary pillars that form the foundation of any fictional culture

| Pillar | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Worldview** | The foundational lens through which a culture operates; "colors everything that culture does" |
| **Religion** | Interwoven with worldview; influences laws, taboos, government types, scientific perspectives |
| **Traditions** | Consistently repeated actions (holidays to daily rituals) that reinforce beliefs |
| **Education** | Systems that transmit knowledge and cultural values; reflects society's priorities |

**Secondary Element:**
- **Government** - Derivative element influenced by and reinforcing all four primary pillars

**Key Insight:** These elements work interdependently, with each reinforcing the others to create cohesive, believable fictional cultures.

### 1.7 Well-Storied's Five Essential Elements

**Source:** [An Introduction to World-Building - Well-Storied](https://www.well-storied.com/blog/an-introduction-to-world-building)

**Approaches:**
- **Inside-Out Method:** Develop core story element first, then determine cascading impacts
- **Outside-In Method:** Build extensive foundational world before story development

**Five Essential Elements:**

**1. Geography**
- Boundaries (continents, countries, planets)
- Water sources (oceans, rivers, lakes)
- Natural features (mountains, forests, deserts)
- Climate patterns and seasonal variations

**2. Cultures & Societies**
- Power structures and hierarchies
- Government systems and laws
- Religious beliefs and practices
- Art, entertainment, cultural expression
- Inter-cultural relations and trade

**3. History**
- "Power Shifts" from social, political, religious upheaval
- Traumatic events (war, famine, plague) with lasting consequences

**4. Magic System** (10 key questions)
- Who wields it, manifestation, control, costs, sources
- Tools, governance, societal perception, types, vulnerabilities

**5. Technology** (10 key questions)
- Communication, travel, daily use, entertainment, affordability
- Combat applications, power sources, regulation, surveillance, societal impact

**Core Principle:** "You only need to develop your world in as much detail as your story requires."

### 1.9 StoryFlint's World Building Bible Structure

**Source:** [How to Create a World Building Bible - StoryFlint](https://www.storyflint.com/blog/world-building-bible)

**Organizational Framework (by question type):**

**Characters ("Who")**
- Major Characters: protagonists and antagonists
- Minor Characters: secondary story roles
- Historical Characters: figures shaping world history
- Deities: gods and goddesses

**Groups**
- Gangs: criminal or revolutionary organizations
- Societies: government or trade-based communities
- Cultures: people sharing histories, traditions, languages
- Institutions: schools, governing bodies, enforcement agencies
- Cults: fanatical religious collectives
- Races: beings with shared physical traits

**Locations and Geography ("Where")**
- Continents, Countries, Regions, States/Provinces
- Cities, Towns, Landmarks
- Roads, Islands, Mountains
- Rivers/Lakes, Seas/Oceans, Wildernesses

**History ("When")**
- Major Historical Events: pivotal moments
- Wars: large-scale political conflicts
- Natural Disasters: floods, eruptions, earthquakes, plagues
- Reigns/Dynasties: ruling periods
- Months: calendar systems
- Eras: distinct historical periods

**World Elements ("What")**
- Flora, Fauna, Religion/Ritual, Mythology
- Items/Artifacts, Language, Systems, Entertainment
- Clothing, Technology, Food/Drink, Disease/Medicine
- Law/Politics, Professions, Astronomy, Weather

**Research**
- Documentation of real-world references supporting fictional development

### 2.2 Orson Scott Card's MICE Quotient

**Source:** [Scott Card's M.I.C.E. Quotient - Writing Excuses](https://writingexcuses.com/writing-excuses-6-10-scott-cards-m-i-c-e-quotient/)

**Framework:** Four fundamental elements present in every story with varying emphasis

| Element | Definition | Focus |
|---------|------------|-------|
| **Milieu** | Landscape, interior spaces, surrounding cultures, weather, laws | Setting/worldbuilding emphasis |
| **Idea** | Information reader discovers during the story | Mystery/revelation emphasis |
| **Character** | Nature of persons in the story - what they do and why | Character development emphasis |
| **Event** | Everything that happens and why | Action/plot emphasis |

**Application:** Short stories typically emphasize one element; novels balance all four.

**Updated Version (Mary Robinette Kowal):** Changed "Idea" to "Inquiry" and added "nesting codes" for structural analysis.

**Flora & Fauna Development**
- Plant adaptations to diverse environments
- Unique animal species with specialized evolutionary traits
- Behavioral patterns and creature interactions

**Interspecies Relationships**

- Symbiosis - dependent partnerships
- Competition - resource partitioning

**Environmental Pressures**
- Natural disasters and recovery mechanisms
- Climate shifts and species adaptation
- Human/civilization impact
- Geological activity

**Energy Flow Systems**
- Food chains (linear energy transfer)
- Food webs (complex interconnected relationships)

**Cultural & Magical Influences**
- Agricultural practices, sacred spaces
- Elemental magic and technological impacts on ecology

## Part 4: Organizational Approaches

### 4.1 Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up

**Top-Down (N.K. Jemisin, RPGBOT):**
- Start with macro elements (cosmos, geography)
- Drill down to specifics (cultures, individuals)
- Better for epic fantasy, sci-fi spanning multiple worlds

**Bottom-Up (Jeff VanderMeer):**
- Start with something living/specific
- Build outward from character experience
- Better for character-driven narratives

### 4.2 Story-First vs. World-First

**Story-First (Brandon Sanderson):**
- Identify story needs
- Develop only relevant worldbuilding
- Avoids "worldbuilder's disease"

**World-First:**
- Complete comprehensive worldbuilding
- Discover stories within the world
- Risk of over-development

### 4.3 Hard vs. Soft Systems

**Hard Systems:**
- Meticulously detailed rules
- Internal logic rigorously maintained
- Reader expects explanations

**Soft Systems:**
- Unexplained, mysterious elements
- Integrated into daily life without exposition
- Maintains sense of wonder

## Part 5: Key Insights and Best Practices

### Consistency Across Sources

1. **Story Service:** Every source emphasizes worldbuilding should serve the story, not overshadow it
2. **Interconnection:** Elements must connect logically (geography affects culture affects economy)
3. **Selective Depth:** Deep development in key areas beats shallow coverage of everything
4. **Internal Consistency:** Rules must be followed once established
5. **Organic Revelation:** Information delivered through character experience, not exposition

### Universal Warnings

- **Worldbuilder's Disease:** Paralysis from over-development (Sanderson)
- **Info-Dumping:** Blocks of detail-heavy narration (multiple sources)
- **Borrowed Worlds:** Over-reliance on existing fictional settings (Scribophile)
- **Disconnected Elements:** Random details lacking coherent connection (multiple sources)

### Framework Selection Guide

| If your story emphasizes... | Consider using... |
|----------------------------|-------------------|
| Epic scope, multiple cultures | Wrede's comprehensive framework |
| Single magic system, tight plot | Sanderson's focused approach |
| Rich cultural depth | Archer's Four Pillars |
| Game/interactive media | RPGBOT's modular structure |
| Series continuity | Bible template approaches |
