## WorldRulesLogic - World Rules and Logic

### Craft Knowledge

**What Are World Rules?**
The fundamental logic and physics of your story world—what is possible, impossible, and how things work.

**Every story has rules**, even contemporary realistic fiction:
- What technology exists
- What social norms apply
- What is physically possible
- What is culturally acceptable

### Types of World Rules

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Physical laws** | Gravity, magic, faster-than-light travel |
| **Social laws** | Class systems, gender roles, legal structures |
| **Economic systems** | Currency, trade, resource distribution |
| **Political structures** | Government, power hierarchies, law enforcement |
| **Technological limits** | What can/cannot be built or done |
| **Magical systems** | What magic can do, its costs, who can use it |

### Key Concepts

**The Contract with Readers**: Establishing rules creates expectations. Violating established rules breaks trust. Consistency is more important than realism.

**Rules Create Story Constraints**: Good rules limit character options in interesting ways. "The constraint is the story." Unlimited power makes boring narratives.

**Sanderson's Laws of Magic**:
1. Ability to solve problems with X is proportional to how well readers understand X
2. Limits are more interesting than powers
3. Expand what you have before adding something new

**Three Main Rules of World-Building (ProWritingAid)**:
1. Goes beyond settings (laws, governance, history, geography, ecology, cultural norms, philosophy, mythology, technology, science)
2. Use other worlds to inspire (borrow from mythology, fairy tales, public domain)
3. Don't make the world the story's focus

**World-Building Questionnaire Categories**:
- Physics/Nature
- Geography
- Magic/Religion
- Population
- Governance

## WorldHistory - World History and Background

### Craft Knowledge

**What Is World History?**
The backstory of your world—events, eras, and changes that created the present state. World history is to world as backstory is to character.

### Functions of World History
1. Explains the present
2. Creates texture
3. Provides conflict sources
4. Supports theme
5. Establishes stakes

### Types of Historical Elements

| Element | Purpose | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **Founding events** | Origin of current order | "The First War established the Three Kingdoms" |
| **Major conflicts** | Source of current tensions | "The elves never forgave the betrayal" |
| **Technological shifts** | Why things work as they do | "After the Fall, magic became rare" |
| **Cultural changes** | Social norms and taboos | "The plague changed how we treat strangers" |
| **Lost knowledge** | Mystery and quest hooks | "The ancient texts were destroyed" |

### The Iceberg Principle
Most world history stays below the surface. The depth below creates authenticity above.

### Revealing World History
**Do**: Weave through dialogue, artifacts, place names, traditions
**Don't**: Lecture readers with "history lessons"

## GeographyCultures - Geography and Cultures

### Craft Knowledge

**Geography Shapes Story**:
- Natural barriers (mountains, rivers, oceans)
- Resources (what people fight over, trade, need)
- Climate (what life is like)
- Travel constraints (journey time, dangers)
- Strategic locations (places worth controlling)

### Geography Shapes Culture

| Geographic Feature | Cultural Impact |
|--------------------|-----------------|
| **Coastline** | Maritime culture, trade, navy, fish diet |
| **Mountains** | Isolation, independence, hardiness |
| **Rivers** | Trade routes, borders, agriculture |
| **Desert** | Nomadism, water rituals, hospitality codes |
| **Forest** | Woodcraft, hunting culture, mysticism |
| **Plains** | Agriculture, cavalry, large settlements |

### Creating Cultures

Cultures develop from:
1. Environment
2. History
3. Resources
4. Neighbors
5. Beliefs

### Cultural Elements to Define

| Element | Questions to Answer |
|---------|---------------------|
| **Social structure** | Who has power? How is it organized? |
| **Values** | What matters to these people? |
| **Customs** | How do they greet, marry, mourn? |
| **Taboos** | What is forbidden? |
| **Aesthetics** | What do they consider beautiful? |
| **Religion/Philosophy** | What do they believe about life/death? |
| **Economy** | How do they make a living? |
| **Technology** | What tools and techniques do they have? |

### Avoiding Stereotypes
- Know the culture through experience or research
- Subvert expectations
- Show internal diversity within groups

## TechnologyMagic - Technology and Magic Systems

### Craft Knowledge

**What Are Technology/Magic Systems?**
Systematic rules for what advanced tools (technology) or supernatural abilities (magic) can do in your world.

**The principle is the same**: Both represent power that characters can wield, and both need rules to create meaningful story.

### Why Systems Need Rules

**Unlimited power = boring story**

Rules create:
- Constraints (what CAN'T be done)
- Costs (what price must be paid)
- Limits (how much, how often, how far)
- Consequences (what happens when misused)

### Sanderson's Laws of Magic

**First Law**: An author's ability to solve problems with magic is proportional to how well the reader understands that magic.
- Soft magic: Mysterious, used for wonder (Gandalf)
- Hard magic: Rule-based, used to solve problems (Allomancy)

**Second Law**: Limitations are more interesting than powers.

**Third Law**: Expand what you have before adding something new.

### Technology System Elements

| Element | Questions |
|---------|-----------|
| **Capability** | What can this tech do? |
| **Availability** | Who has access? |
| **Cost** | What resources does it require? |
| **Side effects** | What are unintended consequences? |
| **Limits** | What can't it do? |
| **Social impact** | How has it changed society? |

### Magic System Elements

| Element | Questions |
|---------|-----------|
| **Source** | Where does power come from? |
| **Access** | Who can use it? Why? |
| **Cost** | What does using it require/take? |
| **Limits** | What can't it do? |
| **Rules** | How does it work mechanically? |
| **Consequences** | What happens when misused? |

### Hard vs. Soft Systems

**Hard systems**: Reader understands mechanics, can solve problems, satisfying when used cleverly
**Soft systems**: Mysterious, creates wonder, can't be used as deus ex machina

### Common Pitfalls
1. No limits
2. Inconsistency
3. Convenience
4. No cost
5. Unexplored implications
