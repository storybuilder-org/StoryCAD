# Worldbuilding Guide Outline

**Purpose:** Educational content for "Writing with StoryCAD" section of user manual
**Audience:** Fiction writers (non-technical, all genres)
**Location:** `/docs/` (user manual)
**Created:** 2026-01-22

---

## Design Principles (from B1-B4 Research)

1. **Lead with "Why"** - Writer benefits, not feature lists
2. **All genres** - Worldbuilding applies to mysteries, romances, thrillers—not just fantasy
3. **Progressive disclosure** - Start simple, add complexity gradually
4. **Story first** - World serves the story, not the other way around
5. **Concrete examples** - Show filled-in examples, not empty forms
6. **Standard terminology** - Characters, Locations, Organizations (avoid jargon)

## Outline

### 1. Introduction: What Is Worldbuilding?

**Goal:** Establish that worldbuilding matters for ALL fiction, not just fantasy/sci-fi.

**Content:**
- Definition: Creating the context where your story takes place
- The "world serves story" principle
- Why consistency matters (readers notice contradictions)
- Worldbuilding for different genres:
  - Fantasy/Sci-Fi: Invented worlds with new rules
  - Mystery/Thriller: The precinct, the courtroom, the criminal underworld
  - Romance: Social settings, cultural expectations, communities
  - Literary Fiction: Time periods, regional cultures, subcultures

**Key Quote:** "World building must not come at the expense of your story." — Jerry Jenkins

**Cross-link:** → Story Elements: StoryWorld

### 2. Two Approaches: Where Do You Start?

**Goal:** Help writers choose their entry point.

**Content:**

**Inside-Out (Story-First)**
- Start with your story idea
- Build world details as needed
- Expand outward from what matters to your plot
- Best for: Writers with a compelling story who need supporting context

**Outside-In (World-First)**
- Start with geography, history, big picture
- Develop cultures, societies, systems
- Find stories within the world
- Best for: Writers passionate about extensive worldbuilding

**Neither is wrong** — choose based on how you think and what excites you.

**Cross-link:** → Story Elements: StoryWorld Structure Tab (World Type)

---

### 3. The Three-Tier System: What to Develop

**Goal:** Help writers prioritize effort and avoid "Worldbuilder's Disease."

**Content:**

| Tier | Description | Development Depth |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Primary** | Elements essential to your plot | Develop thoroughly |
| **Secondary** | Context that answers "Why now? Why them?" | Develop enough for coherence |
| **Tertiary** | Atmospheric details | Sketch lightly or discover while writing |

**Warning Signs of Worldbuilder's Disease:**
- Endless preparation that prevents writing
- Developing histories unconnected to your story
- Prioritizing world over characters and plot

**Key Question:** "If I removed this detail, would my story break?"

### 4. Choosing Your World Type

**Goal:** Explain the Structure tab and World Type classification.

**Content:**

**What kind of world is your story set in?**

| World Type | Description | Examples |
|------------|-------------|----------|
| Consensus Reality | Our world as we know it | Mysteries, legal thrillers, literary fiction |
| Hidden World | Magic/supernatural hidden within our world | Harry Potter, Dresden Files |
| Divergent World | Our world with one key change | Alternate history, "what if" stories |
| Constructed World | Entirely invented world | Lord of the Rings, Dune |
| Broken World | Post-apocalyptic or collapsed civilization | The Road, Hunger Games |
| (others) | ... | ... |

**Why this matters:** Your World Type guides what kind of worldbuilding questions are relevant.

**Cross-link:** → Story Elements: StoryWorld Structure Tab

### 5. Building Your Physical World

**Goal:** Guide writers through geography, climate, and environment.

**Content:**
- Geography: Terrain, landforms, bodies of water
- Climate: Weather patterns, seasons, natural disasters
- Natural Resources: What's available, what's scarce
- Flora & Fauna: Plant and animal life (ordinary or fantastic)
- Astronomy: Moons, stars, celestial features (if relevant)

**For Consensus Reality:** Research real locations; capture specific local character.

**For Constructed Worlds:** Consider how geography shapes civilization.

**Cross-link:** → Story Elements: StoryWorld Physical Worlds Tab

---

### 6. Peoples and Species

**Goal:** Guide writers through populating their world.

**Content:**
- Who lives in your world?
- Physical traits and appearance
- Lifespan and life stages
- Origins and history
- Social structures
- Diversity within the group

**For Consensus Reality:** Think subcultures, professions, communities.

**For Fantasy/Sci-Fi:** Consider multiple species, races, or peoples.

**Cross-link:** → Story Elements: StoryWorld People/Species Tab

### 7. Culture and Milieu

**Goal:** Explain culture as the social environment that shapes characters.

**Content:**
- **Values:** What matters most to these people?
- **Customs:** How they greet, marry, mourn, celebrate
- **Taboos:** What is forbidden or shameful?
- **Art:** How they express themselves aesthetically
- **Daily Life:** Food, clothing, housing, routines
- **Entertainment:** Leisure, sports, games, gatherings

**Key Insight:** Every story has one or more milieus—the social environments where characters exist. Even "realistic" fiction requires understanding the specific culture you're portraying.

**Malinda Lo's Five Foundations (for contemporary fiction):**
1. Power — Who has it, who doesn't
2. Rules — Social expectations and constraints
3. Place — Specific local character
4. Rituals — Social occasions that reveal dynamics
5. Food — Cuisine as cultural identity

**Cross-link:** → Story Elements: StoryWorld Cultures Tab

### 8. Power and Government

**Goal:** Guide writers through political structures.

**Content:**
- Type of government (monarchy, democracy, corporate, tribal...)
- Power structures (who has power, how they keep it)
- Laws and justice
- Class structure and social hierarchy
- Foreign relations (allies, enemies, neutrals)

**Key Question:** How do power dynamics affect your characters?

**Cross-link:** → Story Elements: StoryWorld Governments Tab

---

### 9. Belief and Religion

**Goal:** Guide writers through spiritual/belief systems.

**Content:**
- Deities or higher powers (if any)
- Core beliefs about existence, purpose, afterlife
- Practices and rituals
- Religious organizations and leadership
- Creation myths and sacred stories

**Note:** Even secular worlds have belief systems—philosophies, ideologies, or shared assumptions about how the world works.

**Cross-link:** → Story Elements: StoryWorld Religions Tab

---

### 10. History and Time

**Goal:** Guide writers through developing backstory.

**Content:**
- Founding events: How did the current order begin?
- Major conflicts: Wars, struggles, revolutions
- Eras: Major periods or ages
- Technological/magical shifts: Changes in capability
- Lost knowledge: What's been forgotten?

**Key Insight:** History explains why things are the way they are now. Focus on events that still affect your story's present.

**Cross-link:** → Story Elements: StoryWorld History Tab

---

### 11. Economy and Resources

**Goal:** Guide writers through economic systems.

**Content:**
- Economic system: How does exchange work?
- Currency: What's the medium of exchange?
- Trade routes: How do goods move?
- Professions: What work do people do?
- Wealth distribution: Who has resources, who doesn't?

**Key Question:** How does money (or its equivalent) affect your characters' options?

**Cross-link:** → Story Elements: StoryWorld Economy Tab

---

### 12. Magic, Technology, and Power Systems

**Goal:** Guide writers through "what's possible" in their world.

**Content:**
- System type: Magic, technology, both, or neither
- Source: Where does power come from?
- Rules: How does it work?
- Limitations: What can't it do?
- Cost: What does using it require?
- Practitioners: Who can use it, and why?
- Social impact: How does it affect society?

**Clarke's Law:** "Any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic." — StoryCAD treats magic and technology together because both are power systems with rules and costs.

**Cross-link:** → Story Elements: StoryWorld Magic/Technology Tab

### 13. Putting It All Together

**Goal:** Help writers use StoryWorld effectively.

**Content:**

**Getting Started (Quick Start Exercise):**
1. Pick your World Type in the Structure tab
2. Create one entry in Physical World
3. Create one Culture with basic details
4. Write a short scene using these elements
5. Return to StoryWorld to add details that emerged

**StoryWorld as Your Story Bible:**
- Centralized reference for all worldbuilding decisions
- Maintains consistency across drafts
- Available while outlining scenes

**When to Stop Worldbuilding:**
- You understand what affects your characters
- You can render the world vivid to readers
- You have enough to support your narrative

**Sharing Your World Across Multiple Works:**

A StoryWorld can and should be shared across novels in a series. Your worldbuilding investment pays dividends with each subsequent book:
- Copy the StoryWorld from one story file to the next
- Add new details as the series expands
- Maintain consistency across all books in the series

**Collaborative Fictional Worlds:**

StoryWorld is especially valuable for shared-world fiction where multiple authors write in the same universe:
- Eric Flint's *1632* series (Ring of Fire)
- George R.R. Martin's *Wild Cards*
- Thieves' World anthologies

In collaborative worlds, a shared StoryWorld document becomes the canonical reference that keeps all authors consistent. Each contributor can reference the same Physical Worlds, Cultures, Governments, and History while adding their own characters and stories.

**Cross-link:** → Story Elements: StoryWorld (main page)

### 14. Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Goal:** Help writers recognize and avoid pitfalls.

**Content:**

1. **Worldbuilder's Disease** — Endless preparation that prevents writing
2. **Info Dumps** — Explaining world details that don't serve the scene
3. **Inconsistency** — Breaking your own rules
4. **Irrelevance** — Developing details disconnected from your story
5. **Over-complexity** — Adding systems before you need them

**The Cure:** When you catch yourself worldbuilding instead of writing, start writing. You can always add details later.
